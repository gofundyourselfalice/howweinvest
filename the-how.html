Chapter 2

The How

There are five key steps to investing well. ISAs, funds, what to buy, how much, and how to choose your platform.

  1. Invest tax efficiently
  2. Choose what kind of investments to make
  3. Decide what kind of funds (or stocks) to invest in
  4. Work out how much to invest, and how often
  5. Choose your platform
01

Invest tax efficiently — the power of ISAs

An ISA (Individual Savings Account) is a tax-free "wrapper." It's not the investment itself — it's the account you put your investments in. Think of it like a lunchbox: the ISA is the box, the funds and shares inside it are the food.

With a stocks and shares ISA, you don't pay capital gains tax or dividend tax on anything inside it. You can put up to £20,000 per year into ISAs (the 2025/26 allowance). ISAs cover cash ISAs for risk-averse savers, stocks and shares ISAs for market investing, and Lifetime ISAs that offer a 25% government bonus (up to £1,000 per year) for first-time buyers and retirement.

Important: The £20,000 limit is your total ISA allowance across all ISA types. If you put £10,000 into a cash ISA, you only have £10,000 left for a stocks and shares ISA that tax year.

Also important: From April 2027, the cash ISA limit will reduce to £12,000, but you can invest the other £8,000 in a stocks and shares ISA. This is a government bid to try and encourage more people to start investing — you can still put the full £20,000 in a stocks and shares ISA. Understandably, many people might feel worried about this change, but by the end of this guide, you should be left feeling more confident about investing.

02

Choose what kind of investments to make

There are two main options: stocks and funds. The vast majority of our community invests in funds — for good reason.

A stock is a tiny slice of ownership in one company. If you buy a share in Apple, you own a small piece of Apple. Its value rises and falls with that company's fortunes.

A fund pools money from lots of investors and uses it to buy many stocks at once. Instead of picking one company, you're spreading your money across dozens or hundreds. Less exciting, but much safer — if one company tanks, it barely affects you.

A quick guide to fund types

TypeWhat it is
ActiveManaged by a professional who picks investments. Higher fees, aims to "beat the market." Most don't consistently outperform over the long term.
PassiveTracks an index automatically. Lower fees. This community overwhelmingly favours passive investing.
Index fundA type of passive fund that copies a specific market index (e.g. the FTSE 100 or S&P 500).
ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)A fund that trades on the stock market like a regular share, so you can buy or sell at live prices. Most ETFs are passive — low-cost, easy to trade, and the building blocks of most modern portfolios.

63% invest exclusively in funds. 33% do a mix of funds and stocks. Only 3% pick individual stocks alone.

Because the overwhelming majority invest in funds — and because individual stock picking is very difficult to do well — this guide won't go into stock picking in detail. Bad individual stock picks were also one of the biggest investing mistakes reported in this survey.

03

Decide what kind of funds to invest in

For most beginners, a small number of low-cost, passive index funds will do the job. When we asked our community what they actually invest in, a few names came up again and again — almost all cheap, simple, globally diversified funds. Basic really is best.

A quick note on "Acc" funds. When browsing funds, you'll often see two versions of the same fund: one labelled "Inc" (income) and one labelled "Acc" (accumulation). For most beginners with a long-term horizon, Acc is the one to go for.

Investments pay out dividends — a share of the profits from the companies inside the fund. With an Inc fund, those dividends land in your account as cash. With an Acc fund, they're automatically reinvested. That matters because of compounding — the process by which your returns start generating their own returns. The longer your money is invested, the more powerful this becomes.

Online feature Compounding calculator — "If I invest £X a month for Y years, I'd have approximately £Z." Simple three-input tool, displays result as a chart.

31 funds were mentioned in total. The S&P 500 is the most popular single choice, held by 1 in 4 respondents. Vanguard funds dominate the rest of the top five, followed by the FTSE 100.

What is the S&P 500? An index that tracks the 500 largest publicly traded companies in the United States — Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Tesla, Nvidia. When people say they "invest in the S&P 500," they typically buy a fund or ETF that copies the index, such as Vanguard's VUAG or VUSA. Over the long term, it has historically returned an average of around 10% per year before inflation, though past performance does not guarantee future results.

S&P 500 trackers (25%) — including VUAG (accumulating) and VUSA (distributing). Track the 500 largest US companies.

Vanguard FTSE Global All Cap (10%) — covers the entire global stock market across developed and emerging markets. Over 7,000 companies in one fund.

FTSE All-World / VWRL (8% + 5%) — similar to the Global All Cap but excludes smaller companies. VWRL is the distributing version.

Vanguard LifeStrategy (7%) — a "fund of funds" that mixes stocks and bonds in a set ratio. LifeStrategy 80 means 80% stocks, 20% bonds. A one-stop-shop that handles diversification automatically. Note: LifeStrategy is Vanguard's own concept.

Gold (6%) — held as a hedge against stock market volatility, through physical gold, gold ETFs, or gold mining funds. Worth noting: gold has been behaving atypically recently and isn't the reliable safe haven it has historically been.

The common thread? All passive index funds. They track a broad market, they're cheap to hold, and they require zero stock-picking knowledge. One respondent put it well:

"Vanguard LifeStrategy 80: chose it because I didn't know what I was doing and it seemed safe."

— GFY community member

What people don't want to invest in

Weapons and arms (35%), fossil fuels (24%), and crypto (21%) were the top three avoidance categories. Ethical investing is a priority for our community, not a vague concern.

If ethical investing matters to you too, most platforms now offer ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) filtered funds. They won't be perfect, but they let you align your money more closely with your values.

04

Work out how much to invest, and how often

There's no magic number. What matters more than the amount is the habit.

Most platforms let you start with as little as £1, and many allow regular monthly contributions from £25 or £50. You don't need a windfall or a high salary — you just need to start.

There are two broad approaches.

Lump sum: investing a chunk of money all at once. Historically, lump sum investing tends to outperform drip-feeding money in, simply because your money spends more time in the market. Time in the market beats timing the market.

Pound cost averaging (or drip feeding): investing a fixed amount at regular intervals. Some months you'll buy when prices are high, some when they're low — and over time, those highs and lows average out. It removes the pressure of picking the "right" moment (which even professionals rarely manage) and makes investing feel manageable — just another direct debit.

For most beginners, pound cost averaging is a sensible place to start. It builds the habit and means you're consistently in the market.

The median monthly investment is £200. The most common single answer was £100. The range was enormous: from £5 a month to £35,000 (that last one may have been an annual figure — or we're all very jealous).

The distribution peaks in two places: £150-249 (19%) and £400-599 (20%). When polled on annual totals, 53% invested under £5,000 last year, 35% invested £5,000-£20,000, and 12% invested over £20,000.

There is no right amount — the most important thing is consistency.

You may have noticed that a lot of the information and projections you'll read about investing show a consistent, linear pattern of contributions, which just doesn't reflect the reality of most people's lives. Women in particular are more likely to have to reduce their contributions at different stages of their lives — motherhood can have a huge impact on this, as can a change of career, reduced hours and going freelance. Have honest conversations with your partner, and contribute what you're able — life is messy, and trade-offs can and will need to be made.

For parents: of those who invest for their children, 79% use a Junior ISA.

What is a Junior ISA? A tax-free savings or investment account for under-18s. Parents or guardians can contribute up to £9,000 per year. The child can't access the money until they turn 18.

Of the top platforms in this guide, Vanguard, Hargreaves Lansdown, Moneybox, AJ Bell, and Fidelity all offer Junior ISAs. Trading 212 does not currently.

05

Choose your platform

Three main routes, depending on how hands-on you want to be.

TypeWhat it isPros and cons
Trading / broker / fund platform Do it yourself (DIY). You open an account, choose your own investments, and manage everything yourself. Pros: Full control so more flexibility, likely cheaper depending on your investment choices.

Cons: No specialised guidance: you'll need to be confident making your own decisions.
Financial adviser A qualified professional who assesses your financial picture and recommends specific investments. Typically charges 0.5–1% of your portfolio per year, or a flat fee. Best for complex finances or large sums. Pros: You won't have to think about it, and you'll be dealing with a professional who knows what they're doing.

Cons: More expensive and you'll need to do some research to find one you like and trust.
Robo adviser A digital service that automatically builds and manages a portfolio based on your risk tolerance and goals. Lower cost than a human advisor, less personalised. Pros: Hands-off approach, good for people who don't want to choose their own investments.

Cons: No real advice on your wider finances, and limited flexibility if your needs are more complex. Can be expensive for what is essentially a passive fund strategy.

This is overwhelmingly a DIY community. Most people surveyed prefer to manage their own investments rather than outsource the decision-making.

Providers

  • Trading 212 (31%) — app-first, commission-free. No minimum investment, no trading fees. Offers stocks and shares ISAs, individual stocks, and ETFs.
  • Vanguard (23%) — the go-to for investing directly with a fund provider. Low fees, no individual stock trading (many see this as a feature rather than a limitation).
  • Hargreaves Lansdown (11%) — the largest retail investment site in the UK. Widest range of options. More expensive than Trading 212 or Vanguard, but well regarded for research tools and customer service.
  • Moneybox (9%) — a self-directed robo-adviser. Rounds up spare change from everyday purchases and invests automatically.
  • JP Morgan (was Nutmeg) (9%) — everything from managed portfolios to self-directed investing to dedicated financial advisors.
  • Freetrade (4%) — a trading platform similar to Trading 212.
  • Wealthify (4%) — a robo adviser owned by Aviva, aimed at beginners.
  • AJ Bell (4%) — similar to Hargreaves Lansdown.
  • Fidelity (3%) — similar to AJ Bell and HL, with a wide range of investment options.

The top three alone account for nearly two thirds of all responses. Several respondents use multiple platforms — Trading 212 for their ISA and Vanguard for their pension, for example.
