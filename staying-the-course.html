Chapter 3

Staying the Course

Getting started is one thing. Keeping going when things get uncomfortable is another.

Markets go up and down. That's not a warning, it's just how they work. Every investor, at some point, will log in and see their portfolio in the red. What you do at that moment matters more than almost any other decision you'll make.

When markets drop

Markets have dropped many times. They have recovered every time. The 2008 financial crisis, the 2020 Covid crash, the 2022 inflation shock — each one felt scary in the moment, and they all eventually recovered.

That's not a guarantee of future performance, but it is the full historical record of how stock markets have behaved over the long term, and it is the context that many people miss when they watch their investments fall.

The average time to recover from a major market downturn has historically been two to five years. If your time horizon is ten, twenty, or thirty years, a dip in year two is rarely worth worrying about.

What to do when your portfolio is down

Usually: nothing!

That is the answer for most investors in most downturns, most of the time. You have bought a globally diversified, low-cost index fund. The underlying companies have not disappeared, the market has just moved. Log out and let it recover.

  • Don't sell. Selling locks in a loss. The portfolio is only down on paper until you sell it.
  • Don't check it daily. Checking constantly during a dip serves no useful purpose and makes staying calm harder.
  • Do keep your contributions going. If you invest monthly, keep investing. You are now buying at lower prices, which works in your favour when the market recovers.
  • Do remind yourself of your time horizon. If you don't need this money for ten years, a 20% drop today is not a ten-year problem.

The cost of panic selling

11% of our community cited panic selling during a market dip as their biggest investing mistake.

If you sell during a crash, you cement the loss. If you then wait until things feel "safe" before reinvesting, you will almost certainly buy back in at a higher price than you sold, and miss the recovery entirely.

"Not starting sooner. I was scared of losing money but I've lost more by not investing it."— GFY community
"Withdrawing during Covid and not riding it out."— GFY community

The fear of losing money is valid. But the data from this community is consistent: the people who stayed the course don't regret it. The people who didn't wish they had.

How to check in without obsessing

A quarterly check-in is enough for most passive investors. Things to review:

  • Is your portfolio still broadly in line with your intended fund split?
  • Have your contributions been going in as planned?
  • Has anything in your life changed that affects your time horizon or risk tolerance?

That's it. You don't need to react to monthly market movements. You don't need to rebalance constantly. The point of a passive, diversified fund is that it does most of the work without your intervention.

Turn off daily price notifications if your platform sends them — they're not useful and they're most likely to freak you out.

"Checking it every day, it's long term savings so don't worry about the rise and fall in a day."

— GFY community member

When it is right to make a change

Not all change is panic — there are legitimate reasons to review your investments:

  • Your time horizon has changed significantly (e.g. you now need the money in five years, not twenty).
  • You want to rebalance because one part of your portfolio has grown disproportionately.
  • Your life circumstances have changed (new dependants, approaching retirement, significant change in income).
  • You want to increase your contributions.

These are considered decisions made calmly and as part of your overall plan, not reactions to a market drop on a bad week.
