Chapter 1

The Why

Things we were never taught at school. Why investing matters, when you're ready, and what the community is actually investing for.

In the UK, investing has long been a bit of an enigma. Compared with the US, we are invest-averse: excluding pensions, only 23% of the UK population have invested in the stock market. In the US, it's almost two thirds.

A lot of this comes down to the fact that we don't get taught these things in school. And when we leave, we're up against a world of financial jargon — the finance industry has historically made money sound complicated and scary, and it's no wonder many of us switch off.

A few things worth knowing as you begin

88%
You're already an investor. Around 88% of eligible UK employees are enrolled in a workplace pension, yet only 36% know their pension is invested in the stock market.
11.1%
Cash isn't necessarily safe. UK inflation rose from under 1% to a 41-year high of 11.1% in October 2022, and even at lower rates, cash has consistently lost purchasing power.
£1
There's no such thing as too little. Many people believe the minimum to start investing is far higher than it really is. Women aged 35–54 perceive the minimum for a stocks and shares ISA as more than 50% higher than older women, and people from minority ethnic backgrounds perceive it as 85% higher than the actual minimum.

Stop check: should you invest?

Before we get started, check that you're in a position to invest. Typically, this means:

  • Having paid off high-interest priority debt (student loans and mortgages don't count).
  • Having an emergency fund in place.

A key rule of thumb is that money you'll need within the next five years is better kept in a savings account or cash ISA. Markets go up and down, and a window of less than five years may not give your investments enough time to recover from a dip before you need to access the funds.

Why invest?

Now that you've worked out that you're in a position to invest, it's important to know why you're doing it. Without a clear plan and a time horizon, it's hard to stick with it when markets wobble — and they will wobble.

It's also worth knowing that not investing comes with its own risks.

Money sitting in cash doesn't stand still. Inflation chips away at what you can buy over time. We've all noticed the price of everyday items going up over the last few years. If your savings are earning 4% interest but inflation is running at 3.5%, your money is barely treading water in real terms. Over five, ten, or twenty years, that gap adds up significantly. It's one of the biggest financial risks people don't think about, because it happens slowly and silently.

The longer your time horizon, the more this matters. Investing isn't about getting rich quickly — it's about giving your money the best chance to grow over the long run. Historically, stock markets have outpaced inflation over longer periods, even after accounting for the dips.

The majority of our respondents take a long-term view. That mindset is the right one, because the biggest risk of all might not be investing in a wobbly market. It might be never starting at all.

64% invest outside their pension. The majority use a stocks and shares ISA. The average starting age is 31, though people started anywhere from 17 to 55. If you're reading this thinking "I haven't started yet" — you're in good company. Over a third of respondents don't invest yet either. And 43% of those who do said the number one thing they'd tell their younger self is: just start.

Two-thirds (67%) are investing for retirement or early retirement — whether that's stopping at 65 or the more ambitious FIRE goal of stopping at 45. The underlying motivation is the same: they want to slow down and not work their entire lives. After that, top motivations are security (11%), financial freedom (7%), and property (6%). Very few people said "to get rich quick."

What investing can do for you

It's not just about the money. When asked what investing has enabled them to do, the top answer wasn't "I bought a house" or "I got rich." It went deeper. The top answers were peace of mind (26%) and future planning (27%).

15% said investing gave them career freedom: the ability to leave a toxic job, take a sabbatical, go part-time, or fund a career change.
