What this guide is (and isn't)

Investing, and by investing I mean building your wealth over the long term, has historically been the preserve of finance experts and men in suits. This is changing, but there are still lots of people in the UK who could be making the most of this opportunity. Perhaps one of them is you.

This guide is a window into how normal people (not finance bros, not City workers, not people with trust funds) actually invest. Their words, their numbers, their experiences. It is not a recommendation to do anything in particular. It's your money, your call.

Important disclaimer. This guide is not financial advice. Nothing here is a recommendation to buy, sell, or invest in anything specific. It is a snapshot of what real people said they do with their money, based on anonymous Instagram Story responses collected in March 2026. Every person's financial situation is different. If you want personalised advice, speak to a qualified financial advisor. Investing can help your money work harder, but it comes with risk. The value of your investments can fall as well as rise, and you may get back less than you put in. Only invest what you can afford to leave alone.

The Why

Things we were never taught at school. Why investing matters, when you're ready, and what the community is actually investing for.

In the UK, investing has long been a bit of an enigma. Compared with the US, we are invest-averse: excluding pensions, only 23% of the UK population have invested in the stock market. In the US, it's almost two thirds.

A lot of this comes down to the fact that we don't get taught these things in school. And when we leave, we're up against a world of financial jargon, the finance industry has historically made money sound complicated and scary, and it's no wonder many of us switch off.

A few things worth knowing as you begin

88%
You're already an investor. Around 88% of eligible UK employees are enrolled in a workplace pension, yet only 36% know their pension is invested in the stock market.
11.1%
Cash isn't necessarily safe. UK inflation rose from under 1% to a 41-year high of 11.1% in October 2022, and even at lower rates, cash has consistently lost purchasing power.
£1
There's no such thing as too little. Many people believe the minimum to start investing is far higher than it really is. Women aged 35–54 perceive the minimum for a stocks and shares ISA as more than 50% higher than older women, and people from minority ethnic backgrounds perceive it as 85% higher than the actual minimum.

Stop check: should you invest?

Before we get started, check that you're in a position to invest. Typically, this means:

  • Having paid off high-interest priority debt (student loans and mortgages don't count).
  • Having an emergency fund in place.

Try it · 4 questions

Do you have any high-interest debt? (Credit cards, overdraft, payday loans, not student loans or mortgages.)

Do you have an emergency fund of about 3–6 months of essential expenses?

Will you need this money in the next 5 years? (House deposit, wedding, big purchase.)

Are you OK seeing your portfolio drop temporarily, sometimes 20% or more, and not panic-selling?

Pay off debt first

Knock out the high-interest debt before investing.

Credit card interest of 20%+ eats more than the stock market is likely to give you back. Pay it off first, then come back to this guide. (Student loans and mortgages don't count, different beasts.)

Build your emergency fund first

Park 3–6 months of essential expenses in a savings account.

You don't want to be forced to sell investments at the wrong moment because the boiler died. Build the cushion in a high-interest savings account first, then come back.

Savings account, not investing

Money needed within 5 years lives in cash, not the market.

Markets can be down for years at a time. If you need the money soon, the safest place for it is a cash ISA or high-interest savings account. Anything beyond your 5-year horizon, though, is fair game to invest.

Start small

You're ready, ease in with a smaller monthly amount.

Open a stocks & shares ISA and start with £25–£100/month into a global index fund. Get used to seeing the number go up and down on a regular basis. You can always scale up when you're sure of yourself.

You're ready to invest

Tick, tick, tick, go.

You've cleared the prerequisites: no expensive debt, an emergency fund in place, a long enough horizon, and the temperament to leave it alone. The next step is Chapter 2, the practical bit.

Read Chapter 2 → The How

A key rule of thumb is that money you'll need within the next five years is better kept in a savings account or cash ISA. Markets go up and down, and a window of less than five years may not give your investments enough time to recover from a dip before you need to access the funds.

Why invest?

Now that you've worked out that you're in a position to invest, it's important to know why you're doing it. Without a clear plan and a time horizon, it's hard to stick with it when markets wobble, and they will wobble.

It's also worth knowing that not investing comes with its own risks.

Money sitting in cash doesn't stand still. Inflation chips away at what you can buy over time. We've all noticed the price of everyday items going up over the last few years. If your savings are earning 4% interest but inflation is running at 3.5%, your money is barely treading water in real terms. Over five, ten, or twenty years, that gap adds up significantly. It's one of the biggest financial risks people don't think about, because it happens slowly and silently.

The longer your time horizon, the more this matters. Investing isn't about getting rich quickly, it's about giving your money the best chance to grow over the long run. Historically, stock markets have outpaced inflation over longer periods, even after accounting for the dips.

The How

There are five key steps to investing well. ISAs, funds, what to buy, how much, and how to choose your platform.

  1. Invest tax efficiently
  2. Choose what kind of investments to make
  3. Decide what kind of funds (or stocks) to invest in
  4. Work out how much to invest, and how often
  5. Choose your platform
01

Invest tax efficiently, the power of ISAs

An ISA (Individual Savings Account) is a tax-free "wrapper." It's not the investment itself, it's the account you put your investments in. Think of it like a lunchbox: the ISA is the box, the funds and shares inside it are the food.

With a stocks and shares ISA, you don't pay capital gains tax or dividend tax on anything inside it. You can put up to £20,000 per year into ISAs (the 2025/26 allowance). ISAs cover cash ISAs for risk-averse savers, stocks and shares ISAs for market investing, and Lifetime ISAs that offer a 25% government bonus (up to £1,000 per year) for first-time buyers and retirement.

The £20,000 limit is your total ISA allowance across all ISA types. If you put £10,000 into a cash ISA, you only have £10,000 left for a stocks and shares ISA that tax year.

From April 2027, the cash ISA limit will reduce to £12,000, but you can invest the other £8,000 in a stocks and shares ISA. This is a government bid to encourage more investing, you can still put the full £20,000 in a stocks and shares ISA.

02

Choose what kind of investments to make

There are two main options: stocks and funds. The vast majority of our community invests in funds, for good reason.

A stock is a tiny slice of ownership in one company. If you buy a share in Apple, you own a small piece of Apple. Its value rises and falls with that company's fortunes.

A fund pools money from lots of investors and uses it to buy many stocks at once. Instead of picking one company, you're spreading your money across dozens or hundreds. Less exciting, but much safer, if one company tanks, it barely affects you.

03

Decide what kind of funds to invest in

For most beginners, a small number of low-cost, passive index funds will do the job. When we asked our community what they actually invest in, a few names came up again and again, almost all cheap, simple, globally diversified funds. Basic really is best.

A quick note on "Acc" funds. When browsing funds, you'll often see two versions of the same fund: one labelled "Inc" (income) and one labelled "Acc" (accumulation). For most beginners with a long-term horizon, Acc is the one to go for.

Investments pay out dividends, a share of the profits from the companies inside the fund. With an Inc fund, those dividends land in your account as cash. With an Acc fund, they're automatically reinvested. That matters because of compounding, the process by which your returns start generating their own returns. The longer your money is invested, the more powerful this becomes.

04

Work out how much to invest, and how often

There's no magic number. What matters more than the amount is the habit.

Most platforms let you start with as little as £1, and many allow regular monthly contributions from £25 or £50. You don't need a windfall or a high salary, you just need to start.

There are two broad approaches.

Lump sum: investing a chunk of money all at once. Historically, lump sum investing tends to outperform drip-feeding money in, simply because your money spends more time in the market. Time in the market beats timing the market.

Pound cost averaging (or drip feeding): investing a fixed amount at regular intervals. Some months you'll buy when prices are high, some when they're low, and over time, those highs and lows average out. It removes the pressure of picking the "right" moment (which even professionals rarely manage) and makes investing feel manageable, just another direct debit.

For most beginners, pound cost averaging is a sensible place to start. It builds the habit and means you're consistently in the market.

Staying the Course

Getting started is one thing. Keeping going when things get uncomfortable is another.

Markets go up and down. That's not a warning, it's just how they work. Every investor, at some point, will log in and see their portfolio in the red. What you do at that moment matters more than almost any other decision you'll make.

When markets drop

Markets have dropped many times. They have recovered every time. The 2008 financial crisis, the 2020 Covid crash, the 2022 inflation shock, each one felt scary in the moment, and they all eventually recovered.

That's not a guarantee of future performance, but it is the full historical record of how stock markets have behaved over the long term, and it is the context that many people miss when they watch their investments fall.

The average time to recover from a major market downturn has historically been two to five years. If your time horizon is ten, twenty, or thirty years, a dip in year two is rarely worth worrying about.

What to do when your portfolio is down

Usually: nothing.

That is the answer for most investors in most downturns, most of the time. You have bought a globally diversified, low-cost index fund. The underlying companies have not disappeared, the market has just moved. Log out and let it recover.

  • Don't sell. Selling locks in a loss. The portfolio is only down on paper until you sell it.
  • Don't check it daily. Checking constantly during a dip serves no useful purpose and makes staying calm harder.
  • Do keep your contributions going. If you invest monthly, keep investing. You are now buying at lower prices, which works in your favour when the market recovers.
  • Do remind yourself of your time horizon. If you don't need this money for ten years, a 20% drop today is not a ten-year problem.

The fear of losing money is valid. But the data from this community is consistent: the people who stayed the course don't regret it. The people who didn't wish they had.

How to check in without obsessing

A quarterly check-in is enough for most passive investors. Things to review:

  • Is your portfolio still broadly in line with your intended fund split?
  • Have your contributions been going in as planned?
  • Has anything in your life changed that affects your time horizon or risk tolerance?

That's it. You don't need to react to monthly market movements. You don't need to rebalance constantly. The point of a passive, diversified fund is that it does most of the work without your intervention.

Turn off daily price notifications if your platform sends them, they're not useful and they're most likely to freak you out.

"Checking it every day, it's long term savings so don't worry about the rise and fall in a day."

When it is right to make a change

Not all change is panic, there are legitimate reasons to review your investments:

  • Your time horizon has changed significantly (e.g. you now need the money in five years, not twenty).
  • You want to rebalance because one part of your portfolio has grown disproportionately.
  • Your life circumstances have changed (new dependants, approaching retirement, significant change in income).
  • You want to increase your contributions.

These are considered decisions made calmly and as part of your overall plan, not reactions to a market drop on a bad week.

What comes next

For people who've been investing for a year or two and are ready for the next question.

Rebalancing

Over time, the different parts of your portfolio will grow at different rates. A fund that was 60% of your portfolio might become 70% if it has performed particularly well. Rebalancing means selling a little of what has grown and buying a little more of what hasn't, to bring things back to your intended split.

For most passive investors, this doesn't need to happen more than once a year. Some people do it when one fund drifts more than 5-10% from its target. Others use new contributions to top up whichever fund has fallen behind, rather than selling anything.

While there's no right answer, the main thing is not to let your portfolio drift so far from your original intention that it no longer reflects your risk tolerance.

Consolidating platforms

Many people end up with investments spread across several platforms, often the result of trying different things when starting out. Consolidating into one or two platforms simplifies your financial life and makes it easier to see the full picture.

Before consolidating, check:

  • Whether there are any exit fees on your current platform
  • Whether transferring will trigger a taxable event (transferring between ISAs generally does not, but check)
  • Whether your preferred platform holds all the funds you want to keep

Most platforms offer an in-specie transfer, which means your funds move across without being sold first.

Increasing contributions

The most simple lever most investors have is how much they put in. If your income increases, reviewing your monthly investment contribution is a good habit.

Even moving from £100 to £150 a month makes a meaningful difference over a long time horizon, thanks to compounding.

Beyond the ISA allowance

If you are consistently maxing out your £20,000 ISA allowance, firstly, well done! At that point, the next step for most people is a SIPP (Self-Invested Personal Pension), which offers tax relief on contributions but locks the money away until age 55 (rising to 57 in 2028).

Beyond that, investments held outside an ISA or SIPP wrapper are subject to capital gains tax and dividend tax. The annual CGT allowance is currently £3,000. If you think you might be approaching this threshold, it might be worth speaking to a financial advisor or accountant.

Take this with you

After a year or two, the four levers.

  • Rebalance about once a year, or use new contributions to top up what's lagging.
  • Consolidate platforms when you're sure which one you actually like.
  • Raise your monthly contribution when your income does.
  • When you max out your ISA, look at a SIPP next.

One of the most striking results from the whole survey:

  2. Small amounts are enough (24%)

Where to find trusted information

Instagram and financial creators (16%) narrowly beat financial advisors (13%) as the top information source. YouTube (11%), family and friends (11%), and books (9%) round out the top five. Reddit, TikTok, and even AI tools like ChatGPT all featured.

Social media has opened up financial education, and there are some excellent, trustworthy voices out there. But for every good one, there are plenty who are unqualified, conflicted, or simply wrong. A polished Instagram reel is not financial advice.

Wherever you're getting your information, ask yourself: is this person qualified? Are they regulated? Do they have something to sell me? Are they sharing a balanced view, or just the wins? Treat stock picks, CFDs, crypto "hacks," and anything promising outsized returns with scepticism, no matter how many followers they have.

Use these sources as a starting point, not a shortcut. Read widely, cross-reference what you hear, and for anything that's going to influence a real financial decision, go back to regulated and accredited organisations.

Next steps

Six things to take with you.

Start.

40% said their biggest mistake was waiting. The average starting age is 31, the range 17–55. There is no wrong time.

Start small.

The most common monthly amount is £100. Some people invest £25. The amount matters less than the habit.

Keep it simple.

A stocks & shares ISA. A platform like Trading 212 or Vanguard. A single S&P 500 or global index fund. That's it.

Think long term.

98% of investors don't regret it. The biggest regret is not starting sooner. The biggest benefit is peace of mind.

Stay the course.

Markets will dip. Log out. Keep contributing. The data is on your side.

Invest with your values.

A third of respondents actively avoid certain sectors. You can too, ESG and ethical funds are widely available.
