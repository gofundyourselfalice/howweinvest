What comes next

For when you've been at this a while

For people who've been investing for a year or two and are ready for the next question.

Rebalancing

Over time, the different parts of your portfolio will grow at different rates. A fund that was 60% of your portfolio might become 70% if it has performed particularly well. Rebalancing means selling a little of what has grown and buying a little more of what hasn't, to bring things back to your intended split.

For most passive investors, this doesn't need to happen more than once a year. Some people do it when one fund drifts more than 5-10% from its target. Others use new contributions to top up whichever fund has fallen behind, rather than selling anything.

While there's no right answer, the main thing is not to let your portfolio drift so far from your original intention that it no longer reflects your risk tolerance.

Consolidating platforms

Many people end up with investments spread across several platforms — often the result of trying different things when starting out. Consolidating into one or two platforms simplifies your financial life and makes it easier to see the full picture.

Before consolidating, check:

  • Whether there are any exit fees on your current platform
  • Whether transferring will trigger a taxable event (transferring between ISAs generally does not, but check)
  • Whether your preferred platform holds all the funds you want to keep

Most platforms offer an in-specie transfer, which means your funds move across without being sold first.

Increasing contributions

The most simple lever most investors have is how much they put in. If your income increases, reviewing your monthly investment contribution is a good habit.

Even moving from £100 to £150 a month makes a meaningful difference over a long time horizon, thanks to compounding.

Beyond the ISA allowance

If you are consistently maxing out your £20,000 ISA allowance — firstly, well done! At that point, the next step for most people is a SIPP (Self-Invested Personal Pension), which offers tax relief on contributions but locks the money away until age 55 (rising to 57 in 2028).

Beyond that, investments held outside an ISA or SIPP wrapper are subject to capital gains tax and dividend tax. The annual CGT allowance is currently £3,000. If you think you might be approaching this threshold, it might be worth speaking to a financial advisor or accountant.
