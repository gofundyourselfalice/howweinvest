Story 01 · in her words

“A downturn just means cheaper shares.”

Age
34
Lives
Portugal (via Birmingham & Dubai)
Work
Remote lawyer
Portfolio
~€100,000
Holds
One global ETF (~80% equities / 20% bonds), plus property
Investing since
2020

I'm 34, originally from Birmingham, and after a stint in Dubai I now live in Portugal, working remotely as a lawyer. I'm married, with no children.

It was during Covid that things clicked. With more time to reflect, I realised the money sitting in my bank account was quietly losing value to inflation. I've always carried a bit of anxiety around money, so handing it over to something uncertain felt uncomfortable, but the more I read, the clearer it became: leaving cash in a low-yield account was effectively gambling against inflation.

I didn't grow up in a house that talked about money or investing, so I taught myself. The Psychology of Money by Morgan Housel showed me that doing well with money is about behaviour and mindset, not raw intelligence, and that staying invested beats trying to time the market. The Simple Path to Wealth by JL Collins gave me a clear roadmap: spend less than you earn, avoid debt, and invest the difference in low-cost index funds. Later, podcasts aimed at DIY and female investors reinforced that it doesn't have to be complicated or scary if you're disciplined.

Today I've invested roughly €100,000 into a single global ETF, about 80% equities and 20% bonds. My portfolio is up around 12% since I started, and that's having weathered some real storms. I took significant losses during the early Covid crash and again after the 2020 US election, but I didn't panic-sell. I stayed the course and kept buying. I've never withdrawn a penny of my capital; I'm in it for the long haul, and that discipline has paid off. I also owned a rental property, which I sold last year and reinvested into a second property abroad, to diversify into real estate alongside the market.

Investing has already changed things for me. I've cleared nearly all my personal debt apart from the mortgage, funded our wedding without borrowing, and built a deposit big enough to buy our home in a competitive market, money that doubles as our emergency fund. As a contractor, it also means I could take unpaid maternity leave if I ever needed to, because I know the buffer is there.

I invest to pay off the mortgage, reach financial independence, and live life on our own terms. We love our home and aren't planning to move, so clearing that one payment would be huge. Financial independence, to me, means having enough from investments and assets to support my family and goals without depending on a paycheck.

Honestly, I feel empowered and far more confident than I used to, though I'm always a little nervous. Markets haven't always been kind, and it's hard watching your balance swing so wildly. Right now we've paused our monthly contributions while we fund some home renovations, but I take comfort knowing a substantial amount is still in the market, working for us.

My biggest regret? Not starting sooner. If I'd understood compound interest in my teens or early twenties, it would have made a world of difference. I carried credit-card and student debt into my late twenties with zero financial education, when really I shouldn't have been in debt at all. The penny dropped when I watched friends who'd started tiny, early investments pull far ahead. I delayed, and I paid for it in lost time in the market, the one thing you can never get back.

Shared anonymously with permission. One person's experience, not advice or a recommendation. Capital is always at risk.
